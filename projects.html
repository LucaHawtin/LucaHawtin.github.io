Project 01

Warman Design and Build Competition

A competition designed to challenge students to push the boundaries of mechanical engineering.

The Warman Design and Build Competition is a international competition for engineering students to design and build a robot to complete a specific task. The goal of our years project SEESAW was to design a robot that could successfully navigate the course and deposit balls into designated areas, ultimately achieving a high score in the competition.

I took ownership of the mechanical chassis design and physical fabrication, ensuring our robot could operate efficiently and reliably under competition conditions. The hardest engineering challenge was tuning the physical components to achieve absolute consistency in the robot's autonomous runs. Overcoming this required numerous late nights at university adjusting code, but the final outcome was incredibly rewarding.

Project 02

Wind Tunnel and KidsFest Event

Built and designed a wind tunnel for the KidsFest Event.

I designed and documented a portable, modular wind tunnel for the University of Canterbury (UC) Human Powered Club, created as an interactive exhibit to introduce young minds to engineering. Built for Christchurch's KidsFest and the Children's University outreach programs, the project invites kids to sculpt plasticine vehicles and test them in the tunnel to see who can design the most aerodynamic model.

As the lead designer on the project, I drove the mechanical design and structural documentation from concept to completion. Working with club members to assist with assembly, I engineered a modular three-section chassis, integrated a car radiator fan for airflow, and used a load cell system to measure aerodynamic drag in real-time. The resulting system is fully documented to ensure UC can reliably use it for outreach programs for years to come.

Project 03

Aluminium Assignment

Designed and built an aluminium structure to support a calculated load.

The objective of this project was to design, optimize, and fabricate an aluminum structure tailored to support a highly specific target load with minimal structural weight. Our approach required conducting rigorous hand calculations and structural optimization to determine the most efficient load paths while preventing premature buckling or failure at the joints. The final physical build successfully sustained the calculated design load during destructive testing, verifying our structural model against real-world mechanical behavior.

We focused on the structural stress analysis and final assembly manufacturing, managing our dimensional tolerances so the physical components met the desired tolerances. A major technical highlight was balancing our calculations with ease of manufacture. We intentionally designing out unnecessary geometry to minimise fabrication complexity and risk while ensuring the final structure achieved a highly reliable, and overall optimized result.
